From the **Synthesist-Food-Labs**<br>

<p>I had what felt like a great idea: black walnut Salmiakki -style candy with a licorice twist. The goal was simple—keep it sugar-free using allulose and bring in that classic flavor with anise.</p><br>
<p>I started with a basic AI-assisted recipe and decided to prioritize flavor first. The base was straightforward:
½ cup allulose and 1 tablespoon of water. No surprises there. </p><br>
<p>The problems started when I added 1 teaspoon of anise extract and ½ teaspoon of molasses.
I brought the mixture up to nearly a smoking point before adding the walnuts. At that stage, it didn’t behave like a proper syrup—it was thin—but once it cooled slightly, it looked like it might set.</p><br>
<p>It didn’t.</p><br>
<p>The coating stayed tacky, and the liquid never really evaporated the way I expected. That’s when the experiment turned into recovery mode.</p><br>
<p>For the second round, I adjusted the ratios: <br>
•	Increased allulose to 2/3 cup <br>
•	Added about 2 tablespoons of water <br> 
•	Mixed in roughly 1½ cups of peanuts along with the walnuts <br><br>
This time, the syrup behaved much closer to what I was aiming for. After reheating, the nuts started to take on a more candy-like coating—but still not quite there. The texture remained slightly sticky.</p>
<p>To push it further, I spread the mixture on parchment and baked it at 200°F for another 10 minutes. That helped dry things out a bit. After that, I moved everything to the fridge to cool.
That final step made the candy usable, but not perfect.</p>
<b>What I’d Change Next Time</b><br>
		      
<p>The biggest takeaway: the liquid additions were the problem.
Next round, I’ll simplify: <br><br>
•	Stick with 1 teaspoon of anise (likely oil or a more concentrated form) <br> 
•	Skip the molasses <br>
•	Avoid extra water beyond what’s absolutely needed <br><br>
Allulose behaves differently than sugar—it doesn’t crystallize or dry the same way—so every extra bit of liquid works against you.
This wasn’t a failure, just a sticky iteration. And honestly, the flavor direction is still worth chasing.</p>
